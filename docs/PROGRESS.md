- 2026-07-13 (cont. 2): Flipper power tuning stalled — designer raised torque well past where it made any perceptible difference, then overcorrected into an unstable spin. Sent two parallel research agents (Godot-specific implementations + general flipper-physics/Visual Pinball reference) rather than keep guessing. Both converged on the same finding: a torque+PD-controlled RigidBody2D plateaus because Godot's collision solver derives the ball's impulse from the flipper's actual measured velocity at the exact contact tick, capped by how far torque can accelerate the body within one physics step — more torque past that ceiling doesn't help and destabilizes instead. The fix used by real implementations (Visual Pinball's dedicated flipper solver, and the MIT-licensed reference project `dbisdorf/professor-pinball`) is to drive flipper rotation directly by script over a fixed short duration on a frozen/kinematic body, not via torque at all — a kinematic body's velocity is authoritative to the solver, so it transfers full strength into the ball on contact. Rewrote `flipper.gd` accordingly (eased rotation over `swing_duration_seconds`, `freeze_mode = FREEZE_MODE_KINEMATIC`), which also let us delete the PinJoint2D/center-of-mass workaround entirely — a kinematic body's position is always authoritative, so it can't drift. Added continuous collision detection (`continuous_cd = CCD_MODE_CAST_SHAPE`) to the ball to prevent tunneling at the new higher contact speeds. Verified via a temporary headless probe (removed after use): a well-timed hit produced a ~7x ball speed spike (146 → 997) versus the old torque approach's imperceptible response. Live playtest confirmed: flippers now feel genuinely strong and snappy, designer could hit the ceiling of the current table, which is a good sign for future tables with more vertical space.

## How to Playtest (cabin / any machine)
1. `git pull` in the project folder.
2. Open Godot 4.7, "Import" this folder (project.godot), or just double-click project.godot if Godot is already associated.
3. Press Play (F5) — it should launch straight into the physics prototype scene.
4. Controls: Left/Right arrow keys = flippers, Space = nudge, Up = launch ball (not yet wired to a plunger, ball just starts near the top).
5. What to judge: does the flipper response feel snappy or sluggish? Does the ball ever tunnel through a flipper at speed? Does it feel "right" compared to real/other pinball games? Nothing else matters yet — no art, no objectives, no theme.
